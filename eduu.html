1. Nature & Concept of Research

What research is, its key traits, and what makes it scientific

Simple Definition: Research is like being a detective. You notice something you do not fully understand, ask a clear question about it, gather clues (data), and use those clues to reach a well-supported answer. The goal is to discover reliable, useful knowledge.
🔭
Scientific research follows a systematic plan — like using a telescope to explore the unknown
📊
Data collection and analysis are at the heart of every research study
🏫
Educational research focuses on improving teaching and learning in real classrooms

🔑 Four Key Characteristics of Research

Objectivity

⚖️ No Personal Bias

The researcher does not let personal feelings affect the results. Think of a judge who must decide based on evidence, not emotion. Data — not opinions — leads to the conclusion.

Precision

📐 Exact & Measurable

Results use clear numbers and statistical tools (like averages and standard deviations) so anyone reading can understand exactly what was found. No vague language.

Design

🗺️ A Clear Plan

Every study has a step-by-step plan: define the problem → form a hypothesis → collect data → analyse → report. Like a recipe — skipping a step can ruin the whole dish.

Verifiability

✅ Can Be Checked

Other researchers should be able to repeat the study and confirm (or challenge) the findings. Science builds on itself only when results can be independently verified.

2. Types of Research

Purpose-based, Method-based, and Analysis-based classifications

Research can be grouped in three main ways: what it aims to do (purpose), how it is conducted (method), and what kind of data it uses (analysis type).

🎯 Purpose-Based Classification

Basic / Pure Research

🧬 Building Theory

ExampleStudying how children's brains process language — not to fix a classroom problem immediately, but to expand human knowledge. Like scientists searching for how the universe began.

Applied Research

🔧 Solving Real Problems

ExampleTesting whether a new teaching technique improves exam scores in Yola secondary schools. Directly solves a real, existing educational problem.

Action Research

🏫 Teacher as Researcher

ExampleA teacher notices students struggle with fractions, tries a new strategy, observes results, adjusts, and tries again. It is "learning by doing" in the classroom.

Evaluation Research

📋 Judging Worth

ExampleThe Nigerian government changes from the 6-5-2-3 system to 6-3-3-4 based on evaluation research that assessed the old system's effectiveness.

🔭 Method-Based Classification

MethodWhat It DoesReal-Life Example
ExperimentalManipulates variables with control groups to find cause-and-effectTesting a new drug on one group, not the other
SurveyCollects data from a sample to describe a populationAsking 500 teachers about classroom challenges
HistoricalStudies past events using old records and testimoniesResearching colonial education policy in Nigeria
Case StudyDeep, detailed study of one unit (person, school, community)Studying how one gifted student learns differently
CorrelationalMeasures whether two variables are related (no manipulation)Does study time relate to exam performance?
Ex Post FactoLooks at existing outcomes, then traces possible causes backwardWhy do students from poor homes drop out more?
EthnographyResearcher joins the group being studied (participant observation)A researcher lives with a community to understand their learning practices

📊 Analysis-Based Classification

Quantitative

🔢 Numbers & Statistics

Uses test scores, experiments, questionnaires. Analysed with statistical tools. Example: What is the mean score of 200 students?

Qualitative

💬 Words & Meaning

Uses interviews, observation, documents. Example: How do students feel about online learning?

Mixed Methods

🔗 Both Combined

Uses both approaches for a richer, more complete picture of the topic being studied.

3. The Research Process in Education

Step-by-step journey from a problem to a published report

The research process is like building a house. You cannot start with the roof — you must lay the foundation first, then the walls, then the roof. Each step prepares the way for the next.
  • 1
    Identify the ProblemSomething puzzles or worries you in education. E.g., "Why do girls in Adamawa drop out of secondary school more than boys?"
  • 2
    Review Related LiteratureRead what other researchers have already found about your problem. This prevents repeating old work and reveals gaps you can fill.
  • 3
    Formulate Hypotheses / Research QuestionsMake an educated guess about the answer. E.g., "Girls who receive school feeding are less likely to drop out."
  • 4
    Select Research DesignChoose how you will conduct the study — survey, experiment, case study, etc.
  • 5
    Collect DataGather information using questionnaires, interviews, tests, or observation.
  • 6
    Analyse & Interpret DataUse statistics or qualitative methods to make sense of what you collected.
  • 7
    Discuss FindingsExplain your results in light of past research. Do they agree? Contradict? Extend earlier knowledge?
  • 8
    Draw Conclusions & RecommendationsSummarise what you found and suggest practical actions or further research.
  • 9
    Write the Research ReportPresent everything clearly and formally so others can read, use, and verify your work.

4. Theories in Education

Frameworks that explain how learning happens

Educational theories are like maps — they help teachers navigate the complex terrain of learning. Without a map, you might wander. With one, you make deliberate, effective choices.

🏛️ Major Theory Families — Click to Expand

🐾 Behavioural Theories — Learning through Conditioning ▼

Core Idea: People learn by experiencing consequences. Good consequences (rewards) increase a behaviour; bad consequences (punishments) decrease it.

Classical Conditioning (Pavlov): Pavlov rang a bell every time he fed his dogs. Eventually, the dogs salivated just at the bell sound — no food needed. Classroom example: Students go silent when the principal walks in — not because of a reward, but because of an association built over time.

Operant Conditioning (Skinner): A student gets praise for answering correctly → they answer more often. A student burns their hand on a stove → they never touch it again. Teachers use this when they give stars, grades, or verbal praise.

🧩 Cognitive Theories — Learning through Thinking ▼

Core Idea: The brain is not a passive receiver — it actively processes, stores, and retrieves information like a sophisticated computer.

Piaget's Stages: Children in different age groups think differently. A 5-year-old cannot understand abstract algebra — their brain is not ready. Teachers must match lessons to the child's stage of development.

Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD): What a student can almost do with a little help is the sweet spot for learning. A great teacher stretches students just beyond their comfort zone with support ("scaffolding").

Bandura's Social Cognitive Theory: Children learn by watching others. If a student sees a peer being praised for sharing, they are more likely to share too.

🏗️ Constructivist Theories — Learning by Building Knowledge ▼

Core Idea: Students are not empty containers to be filled with knowledge. They are active builders who connect new ideas to what they already know.

Piagetian Constructivism: Let children handle real objects when learning abstract concepts. A child learning fractions should cut actual oranges before drawing fractions on paper.

Social Constructivism (Vygotsky): Learning happens best in groups. Peer tutoring, group projects, and discussions push students beyond what they could achieve alone.

Bruner's Spiral Curriculum: Teach the same big ideas repeatedly at increasing levels of complexity — like revisiting a town on a spiral road, seeing it from greater heights each time.

❤️ Humanistic Theories — Learning the Whole Person ▼

Core Idea: Students are whole human beings with feelings, needs, and unique potential. Education must address the heart, not just the head.

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs: A hungry child cannot focus on algebra. Before higher learning can happen, basic needs (food, safety, belonging) must be met. A caring classroom atmosphere is not optional — it is foundational.

Rogers' Person-Centred Approach: When teachers listen genuinely and show empathy, students feel safe to take intellectual risks and grow.

🌐 Connectivism — Learning in the Digital Age ▼

Core Idea: In today's world, knowledge is not stored only in books or people's heads — it is spread across networks, the internet, and communities. Learning is the ability to connect to the right information at the right time.

Practical Application: Online collaboration, social media learning groups, personal learning networks (PLNs), and MOOCs (Massive Open Online Courses) all reflect connectivist thinking.

🔢 Multiple Intelligences (Gardner) ▼

Core Idea: Howard Gardner proposed that intelligence is not one thing measured by a single IQ score. There are at least 8 types: Linguistic, Logical-Mathematical, Spatial, Musical, Bodily-Kinesthetic, Interpersonal, Intrapersonal, and Naturalistic.

Real-life example: A student who struggles with written tests may excel in hands-on tasks or music. Good teaching offers diverse ways to demonstrate knowledge.

🎓 Andragogy — How Adults Learn (Knowles) ▼

Core Idea: Adults learn differently from children. They are self-directed, bring rich life experience to the classroom, want to know "why" before they learn "how," and are motivated by real-world relevance.

Application: Master's students like yourself learn best when given autonomy, when lessons connect to your professional problems, and when you can draw on your own experience in discussions.

5. Problem Formulation

How to identify, define, and write your research problem clearly

A research problem is like a diagnosis before a prescription. A doctor who prescribes medicine without knowing what is wrong is dangerous. A researcher who collects data without a clearly defined problem wastes time and resources.

📌 From Broad to Narrow — The Funnel

Research Topic

📚 Broadest Level

Example: "Weapon possession by students in schools" — wide subject area, not yet researchable.

Research Problem

⚠️ The Specific Concern

Example: "Escalating violence in schools due to students carrying weapons" — now we have a specific concern.

Research Purpose

🎯 What You Will Do

Example: "To identify factors that influence the extent to which students carry weapons in high schools."

Research Question

❓ Specific Inquiry

Example: "Do peers influence students to carry weapons to school?"

✅ Characteristics of a Good Research Problem

  • ✔
    Clarity — It is specific and leaves no room for multiple interpretations.
  • ✔
    Relevance — It fills a gap in existing knowledge or addresses a real educational concern.
  • ✔
    Feasibility — Time, money, and data are available to study it.
  • ✔
    Significance — The findings will matter to teachers, policymakers, or students.
  • ✔
    Researchable — It can be answered by collecting and analysing real data.
  • ✔
    Specificity — It is narrow enough to guide the study but not so narrow it limits useful findings.
💡
Topic Selection Tip: Avoid overdone topics (nothing new to discover), topics too controversial for an early researcher, and problems that are either too narrow or too vague. Your topic must have at least two variables and ideally be no more than 25 words long.

6. Hypothesis & Variables

Educated guesses and the things we study

A hypothesis is a well-informed guess about what the data will show. It is not a wild guess — it is based on theory and previous research. Think of it as a weather forecast: based on patterns, you predict rain. You then check if it actually rains.

🔄 Two Main Hypothesis Types

Null Hypothesis (H₀)

❌ No Difference / No Relationship

States that there is no significant effect. Like saying "this new medicine does nothing."
Example: "There is no significant relationship between class size and students' performance in mathematics."

Alternative Hypothesis (H₁ / Hₐ)

✅ There IS a Difference / Relationship

States that something is happening. Can be directional (one-tailed) or non-directional (two-tailed).
Example: "Students who learn through practical application perform better than those who learn theoretically."

📦 Types of Variables

📤 Independent Variable (IV)

The cause. What the researcher controls or changes. Example: Teaching method (traditional vs. inquiry-based)

📥 Dependent Variable (DV)

The effect. What is measured or observed. Example: Students' exam scores

🔀 Intervening Variable

Cannot be seen or measured directly but affects the outcome. Example: Students' motivation, home circumstances, health

🔧 Control Variable

Held constant so it does not interfere. Example: Controlling for gender when studying teaching methods

⚙️ Moderator Variable

Changes the strength of the IV–DV relationship. Example: Socioeconomic status moderating the effect of a school feeding programme

🚫 Extraneous Variable

Unwanted variable that could distort results if not controlled. Example: Noise in the research environment

7. Techniques for Literature Review

How to find, read, and use what others have already discovered

Imagine you are going to dig a well. You first ask neighbours where they found water and how deep they dug. That is what a literature review does — it tells you what others have already found so you do not repeat their mistakes or their work.

🔍 Why Literature Review Matters

🧭 Identifies Gaps

Shows what has not been studied yet — your opportunity to contribute something new.

📖 Gives Background

Provides the theoretical and empirical context in which your study sits.

🔬 Suggests Methods

Shows which research designs and tools others used — guiding your own choices.

💬 Supports Discussion

When you discuss your results, you compare them to earlier findings from the review.

📁 Sources of Literature

Books, journal articles, theses and dissertations, government publications, conference papers, newspapers, encyclopaedias, abstracts (e.g., ERIC — Educational Resources Information Centre), and reliable internet sources. Always prefer primary sources (original research) over secondary ones (summaries by others).

🔑 Conceptual vs. Theoretical Framework

Theoretical Framework

🏗️ The Foundation — Other Experts' Ideas

This is what established scholars (like Vygotsky, Piaget, Bandura) have already said about your research area. You stand on the shoulders of giants.

Conceptual Framework

🗺️ Your Master Plan — Your Own Logic

This is your personal blueprint for the entire study: what you are studying, why, how, and with what tools. It is the "big picture" map of your research.

8. Research Design

The blueprint for how your study will be structured and conducted

A research design is like an architect's blueprint. Before a building is constructed, every detail — rooms, doors, electricity — is planned on paper. A research design plans how data will be collected, from whom, when, and how it will be analysed.

🗄️ Five Key Types of Research Design

Descriptive

🖼️ Painting the Picture

Describes what exists. No manipulation of variables. Example: A demographic profile of dropout students in Adamawa State schools.

Correlational

🔗 Finding Connections

Measures whether two variables move together. Does NOT prove causation. Example: Is time spent studying correlated with exam scores?

Experimental

⚗️ Testing Cause and Effect

Manipulates an IV and measures effect on DV with control and experimental groups. Random assignment is the gold standard.

Quasi-Experimental

🔬 Experiment Without Full Control

Like experimental, but without random assignment. Uses existing groups. Common in educational settings where random assignment is not ethical or practical.

Explanatory

❓ Asking "Why"

Explores poorly understood phenomena to discover underlying causes. Uses literature review, interviews, and focus groups.

⚗️ Experimental Design Types

DesignFeaturesWeakness
Pre-Experimental (One Group Pre-Post)One group, measured before and after treatmentNo control group — hard to know if results are due to the treatment
True Experimental (Randomised)Random assignment to control and experimental groupsNot always feasible in real classrooms
Solomon Four-GroupFour groups — two with pre-tests, two withoutComplex and resource-intensive
Quasi-Experimental (Non-Equivalent Groups)Existing groups used without random assignmentPre-existing differences between groups can bias results
Factorial DesignStudies multiple IVs simultaneously and their interactionsComplex to analyse and interpret

⚠️ Threats to Validity

Internal Validity

Threats Inside the Study

Selection bias, maturation (students grow older), testing effects (familiarity with tests), history effects (outside events), instrumentation changes.

External Validity

Can Results Generalise?

Population differences (results apply only to similar groups), ecological validity (lab results may not apply to real classrooms), temporal validity (results may change over time).

9. Instrumentation

Tools for collecting research data — questionnaires, interviews, tests and more

An instrument is the tool a researcher uses to collect data. Just as a doctor uses a thermometer to measure temperature, a researcher uses a questionnaire, interview schedule, or test to measure variables.

📝 Main Data Collection Tools

Questionnaire

📋 Written Questions

A set of written questions answered by respondents. Can be structured (fixed options) or unstructured (open-ended). Best for large samples. Must be clear, unbiased, and pre-tested.

Interview

🗣️ Face-to-Face

Researcher speaks directly with participant. Can be structured, semi-structured, or unstructured. Provides rich, detailed data. Builds rapport. Expensive and time-consuming for large groups.

Observation

👁️ Watching in Context

Researcher watches behaviour as it naturally occurs. Can be overt (participants know) or covert (they do not). Used with checklists and direct observation forms.

Focus Groups

👥 Group Discussions

6–10 participants discuss a topic guided by a facilitator. Provides rich qualitative data quickly. Risk: vocal members may dominate quieter ones.

Achievement Tests

📊 Measuring What Was Learned

Tests what participants know or can do at a given time. Must be reliable (consistent), valid (measures what it claims to), objective (unbiased scoring), and usable.

Schedules

📄 Interviewer-Held Forms

A list of questions the interviewer asks and fills in — unlike a questionnaire the respondent fills in themselves. Used in face-to-face or phone interviews.

📏 Levels of Measurement

ScaleWhat It MeasuresExampleAllowed Statistics
NominalCategories only — no orderGender (Male/Female), ReligionMode, Chi-square
OrdinalRank order, but unequal gaps1st, 2nd, 3rd in classMedian, Percentiles
IntervalEqual gaps, no true zeroTemperature (°C), IQ scoresMean, SD, Correlation
RatioEqual gaps + true zeroAge, Income, Exam marks out of 100All statistics including geometric mean

🔒 Reliability & Validity

Reliability

🔁 Consistency

If you weigh yourself on the same scale three times and get the same result each time, the scale is reliable. Types: Test-retest, Inter-rater, Internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha), Parallel forms.

Validity

🎯 Accuracy

Is the instrument actually measuring what it claims to measure? A scale that consistently shows 50kg when you weigh 70kg is reliable but NOT valid. Types: Construct, Content, Face, Criterion validity.

⚠️
Important: A measure can be reliable without being valid, but it cannot be valid without first being reliable. Reliability is necessary but not sufficient for validity.

10. Population, Sampling & Sampling Techniques

How to select who participates in your study

You cannot interview every student in Nigeria. Instead, you select a sample — a smaller, representative group — and use the results to draw conclusions about the larger population. This is like tasting a spoonful of soup to judge the whole pot.

🎲 Probability Sampling (Every Unit Has a Known Chance)

Simple Random Sampling

🎰 Like a Lottery

Every person in the population has an equal chance of selection. Draw names from a hat, use a random number table, or a computer random generator.

Systematic Sampling

📏 Every kth Person

Select every 5th (or 10th, 20th) person from a list. Example: From a list of 1,000 students, select every 10th — giving 100 participants.

Stratified Sampling

📦 Divide Then Sample

Divide the population into subgroups (strata) by gender, class, etc., then sample from each stratum. Ensures all subgroups are represented.

Cluster Sampling

🗺️ Sample Groups Not Individuals

Randomly select whole schools, villages, or districts. Then study everyone (or a sample) within each selected cluster. Useful when population is geographically spread.

Multi-Stage Sampling

🔗 Sampling in Stages

Combines techniques: first select states → then LGAs → then schools → then students. Used for large national surveys.

🎯 Non-Probability Sampling (Selection Not Random)

Convenience Sampling

🏃 Whoever Is Available

Select participants who are easiest to access. Example: Surveying only students in your own department. Results cannot be generalised broadly.

Purposive Sampling

🎯 Select by Criteria

Choose participants who meet specific criteria for the study. Example: Only physics teachers with more than 10 years of experience.

Snowball Sampling

⛄ Participants Recruit Others

Start with a few participants who then recommend others. Useful for hard-to-reach groups like school dropouts or people with rare conditions.

Quota Sampling

🧮 Fill a Quota

Set targets for each subgroup (e.g., 50 males, 50 females) and recruit until quotas are filled. Not random, so results are less generalisable.

💡 Remember Probability Sampling Types: "Simple Systematic Strategies Create Multi-layer Plans"

Simple Random  |  Systematic  |  Stratified  |  Cluster  |  Multi-stage

11. Data Analysis & Interpretation

Making sense of what you collected

Data without analysis is like ingredients without a recipe. Analysis transforms raw numbers and words into meaningful conclusions that answer your research questions.

📉 Descriptive Statistics — Summarising Your Data

Mean

➗ The Average

Sum of all values ÷ number of values. Most common measure of the centre. Affected by extreme scores (outliers).

Median

⬛ The Middle Value

The value that splits data exactly in half. Less affected by outliers than the mean. Better for skewed data.

Mode

🏆 The Most Frequent

The value that appears most often. Used for nominal (categorical) data where mean and median make no sense.

Standard Deviation

📏 Spread from the Mean

How far scores typically are from the average. Low SD = data clustered close together. High SD = data spread far apart.

📐 Inferential Statistics — Drawing Conclusions

TestWhen to UseExample Research Question
t-test (Independent)Compare means of 2 groupsDo boys and girls score differently in physics?
t-test (Paired)Compare same group at 2 time pointsDid scores improve after the intervention?
ANOVA (F-test)Compare means of 3+ groupsDo students in Junior, Senior, and Adult classes differ in learning motivation?
Pearson CorrelationRelate 2 continuous variablesDoes study time relate to exam scores?
Multiple RegressionPredict DV from multiple IVsWhat factors predict academic performance?
Chi-squareCompare categorical frequenciesIs gender related to school dropout?
Mann-Whitney UNon-parametric alternative to t-testUsed when data is not normally distributed

🔑 How to Interpret Results

📌
The p-value rule: If p ≤ 0.05, the result is statistically significant — reject the null hypothesis. If p > 0.05, do not reject it. In SPSS output, look at the column labelled Sig. — values of 0.05 or less mean a significant finding.
📌
Correlation strength (Cohen, 1988): r = .10–.29 = Small | r = .30–.49 = Medium | r = .50–1.0 = Large. A negative r (e.g., r = −0.65) is still a strong relationship — the minus sign tells you the direction, not the strength.

12. Research Report Writing & APA Referencing

Communicating your findings professionally

A research report is the final product of months or years of work. Writing it well means others can understand, use, and build on your findings. Modibbo Adama University, Yola uses APA (American Psychological Association) referencing style.

📖 Structure of a Thesis by Chapters

  • 1
    Chapter One: IntroductionBackground to the study, statement of the problem, objectives, research questions/hypotheses, significance, scope and limitations, definition of terms.
  • 2
    Chapter Two: Review of Related LiteratureTheoretical framework, conceptual framework, review of empirical studies related to your topic. Concludes by identifying the gap your study fills.
  • 3
    Chapter Three: MethodologyResearch design, area of study, population, sample and sampling technique, instruments, validity, reliability, data collection procedure, and data analysis plan.
  • 4
    Chapter Four: Results, Analysis & DiscussionPresents data in tables/graphs, answers each research question or tests each hypothesis, discusses results in light of previous literature.
  • 5
    Chapter Five: Summary, Conclusions & RecommendationsSummarises the whole study, draws conclusions, states limitations, and makes recommendations for practice and future research.

📌 APA Citation — Quick Guide

One Author In-Text

Smith (2020) found...

OR at the end: (Smith, 2020)

Two Authors

Johnson and Williams (2015)

In parentheses: (Johnson & Williams, 2015) — use "&" inside brackets, "and" in text.

3–5 Authors (1st cite)

Kernis, Cornell, Sun, Berry, and Harlow (1993)

Subsequent citations: Kernis et al. (1993)

Reference List (Book)

Format

Smith, J. A. (2001). The art of writing. Academic Press.

Reference List (Journal)

Format

Johnson, M., & Williams, K. L. (2015). The impact of technology on education. Journal of Educational Research, 108(3), 214–226.

Null Hypothesis (H₀)

p-value Decision Rule

If computed p ≤ 0.05 → Reject H₀ (significant). If p > 0.05 → Fail to reject H₀ (not significant).
